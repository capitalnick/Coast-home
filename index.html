The lifestyle.

Dalmeny is a small, kind, coastal town just north of Narooma on the NSW South Coast. Population a touch over 1,500. The kind of place where the IGA owner knows your face by week three, where the morning walk ends with toes in the sand, and where the noisiest thing in the street is the wind through the casuarinas.

What is on your doorstep

400 M

The beach.

Yabbarra Beach is a five-minute walk. Protected swimming, golden sand and dune walks. Brou Beach and Mummaga Lake are just as close. Head north past Mummaga Lake and you can walk all the way to Potato Point.

21 KM RETURN

Cycleway to Narooma.

A shared coastal path along Wagonga Inlet and Mummaga Lake, all the way to Narooma. Flat and easy. The Mill Bay boardwalk at the southern end is worth a stop; seals and seabirds are regulars. E-bike hire available from Southbound Escapes in Narooma.

VILLAGE

Eat, drink, done.

IGA, fuel, post and a progressive food scene for a town of 1,500. Sea Salt cafe for breakfast. Big Niles Brewing Co for a cold one; their Harry's Pale Ale is award-winning. Everything you need; no driving for milk.

LOCAL

Community.

Bowls club, fishing club, the campground in the middle of town. Apex Park and the boat ramp are a regular hangout. A steady rhythm to the year, and locals who actually know your name.

Seven kilometres up the road: Narooma & Barunguba

Narooma is the next town up. Wagonga Inlet, the famous Australia Rock, the golf course with a hole right on the water, and a working harbour that runs charters out to Barunguba (Montague Island). A 20-minute boat ride gets you to one of the most extraordinary marine spots in the country.

Dalmeny sits at the northern end of Tourist Drive 5, a scenic route that winds south through headlands with 180-degree ocean views and prime whale-watching vantage points during the spring migration. Drive it slowly; there are picnic spots, lookouts and stretches of coastline that earn a proper stop.

The arrangement.

This is the framework, not the contract. The exact ownership structure, loan structure and tax treatment all need to be confirmed by a solicitor and an accountant before anything is signed. Here is what the shape looks like.

Sized to clear lender requirements. He owns equity in the property from day one.

3

Income split 40 / 40 / 20.

Forty per cent to Graeme, forty per cent to a maintenance fund, twenty per cent to the mortgage offset.

The 40 / 40 / 20 split, explained

40% to Graeme.

His deposit is the reason this property exists. He gets a real, ongoing return on that capital, paid quarterly into his account.

40% to a maintenance fund.

A coastal property in salt air is a beast that wants feeding. Painting, gardens, the spa, the unexpected. Anything not spent stays in the fund and means surprises don't become arguments.

20% to the mortgage offset.

Goes straight against the home loan. Reduces interest paid by Nick & Jodie, and grows everyone's equity faster.

Things to nail down

  • Ownership structure.
    Tenants in common, with specific shares, is usually the right vehicle here. Not joint tenancy.
  • Co-ownership agreement.
    Written, signed, covering: who pays what (rates, insurance, repairs), how decisions get made, what happens if someone wants out, dispute process.
  • Right of residence.
    A documented right for Graeme to live in the granny flat. His ability to live there should not depend on goodwill alone.
  • Estate planning.
    Wills and beneficiary nominations need to be aligned with the arrangement. A solicitor can sort this in an afternoon.
  • Tax & Centrelink.
    Capital gains treatment, Graeme's pension/assets-test position, and any deemed-rent issues need an accountant's eye before signing.
  • Exit & sale terms.
    Pre-agreed valuation method and a fair process if anyone needs to sell, or if circumstances change.
What this is not:
  • A guarantee. Booking rates and rental markets fluctuate.
  • A substitute for legal advice.
  • A pension test workaround. Graeme should check Centrelink implications.
